# Strategy

## The Plan — How Commercial Strength Funds Civilizational Defense

This document answers the question: *given where we are in 2026, how do we actually build this, in what order, with what resources?*

It is a strategy, not a prophecy. It will be revised as reality demands. What will not be revised is the **principle that drives the strategy**: *quwwat first, hifazat follows* — build capacity, then apply capacity to defense.

---

## The Core Strategic Insight

A pure defense project (only atrocity documentation, counter-surveillance, accountability audits) has three structural weaknesses:

1. **Funding is always precarious.** Grants run out. Foundations change priorities. Political winds shift.
2. **Scale is always limited.** Without commercial distribution, defensive tools reach only the technically literate or the already-activist. The 95% of ordinary Muslims never hear about them.
3. **Burnout is chronic.** Mission-driven work without commercial sustainability exhausts its contributors. Two to five years is the typical lifecycle.

A pure commercial project (only scholarly AI, no defensive mission) has different weaknesses:

1. **Mission drift is inevitable.** Investors, customers, and growth metrics gradually reshape priorities. Ten years in, the company is not what it said it would be.
2. **Principled refusals get costly.** When a lucrative customer wants something the principles forbid, commercial pressure pushes toward compromise.
3. **Civilizational impact is limited.** A successful commercial AI company serves its customers well; it does not necessarily build defensive capacity for the vulnerable.

**The synthesis is a dual-track structure:**

- **Track A (commercial):** Basira and potentially other commercial products generate revenue, build distribution, and attract talent.
- **Track B (mission):** Shahid, Hisn, Mizan, and other defensive projects do the work that commercial logic would never fund directly.
- **A structural commitment** — built into governance from the beginning — routes resources from Track A to Track B permanently.

This is the Ummah's own institutional form: the `waqf` (perpetual endowment). Track A is the revenue-generating asset. Track B is the beneficiary.

## The Five-Phase Roadmap

### Phase 0 — Foundation (Now through mid-2026)

**Goal:** Establish the principles, architecture, and initial community so that future work has a strong base.

**Activities:**
- Complete foundation documentation (this repository).
- Open the initiative publicly. Invite first contributors.
- Establish governance scaffolding.
- Build relationships with 5–10 initial scholarly advisors across sects and schools.
- Identify and approach 3–5 potential institutional partners (Muslim research institutions, digital rights organizations).
- Set up infrastructure: open-source repositories, communication channels, baseline legal structure.

**Budget:** Founder's time. Minimal cash ($500–2000 for domains, hosting, legal formation).

**Exit criteria for this phase:**
- Foundation documents published and stable.
- At least 5 active contributors (technical, scholarly, or organizational).
- Legal entity established in an appropriate jurisdiction.
- Initial scholarly advisory group convened.

### Phase 1 — First Product (Mid-2026 through mid-2027)

**Goal:** Launch Basira MVP and prove the model.

**Activities:**
- Build Basira MVP: Quran + Hadith (6 major Sunni + 4 major Shia collections) + select tafseer. Arabic + Urdu + English initially.
- Establish scholar-verification process for production content.
- Release evaluation benchmarks (Layer 1, open) for Islamic LLM accuracy.
- Launch free tier publicly.
- Begin limited B2B conversations (madaris, universities, Islamic education platforms).
- Publish first version of Basira tooling (Layer 2, open) for other developers to build on.

**Budget:** $5K–30K out-of-pocket or bootstrapped from early revenue. Avoid venture funding this phase.

**Exit criteria:**
- 10,000+ active users on free tier.
- 3+ paying institutional customers or $5K+ MRR.
- Published evaluation benchmarks and at least one open-source tooling release.
- Scholarly advisory has reviewed and endorsed the verification process.

### Phase 2 — First Defensive Project (Mid-2027 through mid-2028)

**Goal:** Launch Shahid (or Hisn, whichever is more tractable at the time) as the first dedicated defensive project, funded by Basira revenue.

**Activities:**
- Commit waqf-style revenue share: minimum 20% of Basira net revenue directed permanently to Track B (mission/defensive work). This commitment is:
  - **Contractually binding** in the organization's governing documents (articles of incorporation, bylaws, or charter), not a discretionary allocation.
  - **Calculated on net revenue** after direct operating costs, before discretionary reinvestment — so the mission share cannot be starved by growing the cost base.
  - **Non-reducible** without a full governance process including the mission guardians, with strong presumption against reduction.
  - **Audited annually** by an independent party, with the audit published.
  - **Tracked transparently** — users, contributors, and the public can see what is allocated and what it funded.
  - **Applicable even in loss years** — where there is no net revenue, the commitment rolls forward, and when profitability returns, arrears are made up before other allocations.
- Form partnerships with existing human rights organizations (HRW, 7amleh, Bellingcat, Access Now).
- Build Shahid intake and verification infrastructure.
- Publish first investigations: likely AI-driven targeting and surveillance case studies.
- Establish legal support channels for cases where Shahid documentation supports litigation or formal accountability.

**Budget:** 20% Basira revenue + grant funding (target: $100K–500K via digital rights foundations).

**Exit criteria:**
- Shahid or equivalent defensive project live with documented case studies.
- At least one formal partnership with an established human rights organization.
- Documented legal or journalistic use of Shahid's archive.
- Sustainable funding mix between Basira allocation and independent grants.

### Phase 3 — Ecosystem (Mid-2028 through 2030)

**Goal:** Grow from a single organization into a genuine ecosystem.

**Activities:**
- Launch Hisn and Mizan as independently-contributed projects.
- Open contribution channels so that other builders can start sub-projects under the umbrella.
- Support emerging commercial products built on Basira's open tooling — other Islamic AI companies, each potentially with its own commercial model but using shared infrastructure.
- Expand language coverage: Urdu, Arabic, Bahasa, Malay, Bengali, Persian, Turkish, at minimum.
- Establish a formal Muslim AI developer community (conferences, forums, training).
- Begin serious work on sovereign hosting infrastructure (independent of US hyperscalers).

**Budget:** Basira scaled to $1M–10M ARR with 20%+ directed to ecosystem. Grant and institutional funding for specific projects. Potentially strategic investment from Gulf/Malaysian/Indonesian mission-aligned capital if terms are acceptable.

**Exit criteria:**
- At least four projects in the portfolio (all founding four) with active contributors.
- 100+ active developers in the Muslim AI developer community.
- Measurable policy or platform impact from Mizan audits.
- Demonstrated defensive impact: documented cases where Hisn's tooling or Shahid's archives protected or supported individuals.

### Phase 4 — Infrastructure Sovereignty (2030 and beyond)

**Goal:** Move from software-layer work to deeper infrastructure — hosting, compute, foundation models — that reduces Ummah's dependence on external infrastructure over time.

This phase is deliberately vaguer because it will depend heavily on what has emerged by 2030 (Gulf sovereign AI funds, Malaysian/Indonesian capacity, unforeseen developments). The commitment at founding: we begin planning Phase 4 in Phase 3, so that we are not surprised when the opportunity arrives.

## Funding Strategy

### Primary principle: the smallest acceptable capital

Capital has conditions. Conditions shape behavior. Minimum viable capital maximizes mission integrity.

**Preferred order of funding sources:**

1. **Founder's own resources** — where feasible.
2. **Customer revenue** — Basira's own earnings.
3. **Waqf / endowment-style gifts** — from mission-aligned donors who accept that they have no operational control.
4. **Grants from mission-aligned foundations** — digital rights, press freedom, Muslim philanthropic trusts.
5. **Strategic capital from mission-aligned investors** — Gulf family offices, Malaysian sovereign funds, Muslim-world institutional investors. Only if terms preserve mission integrity.
6. **Conventional venture capital** — last resort. Likely never a fit because VC expects returns on timelines incompatible with a civilizational project.

**Explicitly refused:**
- Capital from entities with documented roles in AI-enabled harms (military-tech conglomerates, certain surveillance firms).
- Capital from any entity that requires a seat at the table in decisions that would violate principles.
- Capital whose terms include non-disclosure clauses that conflict with transparency principles.

### Budget Targets (Rough, 2026-Era Estimates)

| Phase | Duration | Cumulative Budget | Primary Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (Foundation) | ~6 months | $1-5K | Founder |
| 1 (Basira MVP) | ~12 months | $10-30K total | Bootstrap, early revenue |
| 2 (First defense project) | ~12 months | $200-500K cumulative | Revenue + grants |
| 3 (Ecosystem) | ~24 months | $2-10M cumulative | Revenue + grants + strategic |
| 4 (Infrastructure) | 2030+ | TBD | To be determined |

These are rough targets. Real figures will be documented as the project progresses.

## Talent Strategy

**Year 1:** Founder + 1-2 volunteer contributors.
**Year 2:** 3-5 part-time contributors, possibly 1-2 part-time paid roles (scholar reviewer, senior engineer).
**Year 3:** Small full-time team (3-6 people) as revenue allows.
**Year 5:** Federated team across projects, potentially 15-30 total contributors (full-time and part-time) across the portfolio.

**Recruitment priorities:**
1. Technical excellence.
2. Principles alignment — demonstrated, not asserted.
3. Pluralism — actively build a team that spans sects, nationalities, genders.
4. Stability — we hire people who want to build for a decade, not people looking for the next hot thing.

**Compensation:**
- Competitive where we can afford it.
- Transparent — pay structures are documented.
- Equity where relevant (for commercial subsidiary), without creating internal inequality that destroys culture.

## Partnership Strategy

### Who we want to work with

- **Scholarly institutions:** Madaris, Islamic universities, research institutes.
- **Human rights organizations:** HRW, Amnesty, 7amleh, Access Now, Article 19, EFF.
- **Academic partners:** Digital rights labs, Middle East studies programs, Islamic studies departments, computer science departments with aligned ethics focus.
- **Muslim developer communities:** Regional Muslim tech associations, diaspora tech groups.
- **Media partners:** Outlets that cover these issues with integrity.

### Who we will not work with

- Governments or regimes with documented civilian targeting records, regardless of offer.
- Corporations whose core business conflicts with our principles.
- Any entity requiring opacity we cannot accept.

### Partnership form

- Default public. Partnership terms are disclosed.
- Non-exclusive. We do not accept lock-in that prevents serving other aligned partners.
- Values-aligned contractual terms. No partner can require us to violate a principle.

## Risk Register

Major risks and mitigations:

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Founder dependence / bus factor | High (Phase 0-1) | Catastrophic | Early public documentation; early contributor recruitment; governance that doesn't require founder |
| Capture by sectarian interest | Medium | Severe | Non-sectarian principle; pluralistic advisory; fork rights |
| Capture by commercial pressure | Medium-High | Severe | Revenue-share commitment; board structure with mission guardians; transparent decision-making |
| Capture by state actor | Medium | Severe | Jurisdictional diversification; zero-knowledge architecture for sensitive data; transparent operations |
| Scholarly reputational damage | Medium | Severe | Multi-school verification; transparent methodology; scholar-of-the-month confirmation of content quality |
| Technical security breach | Medium | Severe | Layered disclosure framework strictly applied; security audits; bug bounties once mature |
| Burnout / founder breakdown | Medium (Phase 0-1) | Severe | Sustainable pace expectation; family and faith obligations prioritized; explicit "no hero mode" culture |
| Fundraising failure | Medium | Moderate | Bootstrap-first strategy; multiple funding tracks; acceptance that slower growth is acceptable |
| Legal / regulatory attack | Low (Phase 0-2), Rising later | Severe | Jurisdiction selection; legal counsel engaged as project grows; transparent operations as defense |

This register is updated periodically. New risks are added as they emerge.

## Decision Heuristics

When facing a strategic choice and principles do not clearly settle it, these heuristics guide:

1. **Will this decision look right in ten years, even if it looks wrong in ten months?**
2. **Does this decision preserve our ability to make future decisions freely?**
3. **Does this decision increase or decrease our dependence on external parties?**
4. **Would we make this decision if no one was watching?**
5. **Does this decision expand or narrow the coalition we can work with?**
6. **Is this decision reversible? If not, are we sure?**

Prefer: long-horizon, freedom-preserving, dependency-reducing, principled, coalition-expanding, reversible.

## Closing

This strategy is a framework, not a script.

The specific decisions — which customer to accept, which grant to pursue, which feature to build, which team member to hire — will be made by the people on the ground at the time, informed by principles, guided by this strategy, and accountable to the community.

If the strategy turns out to be wrong, we revise it. If the principles turn out to be wrong — they do not. They are our commitment.

The test of the strategy is whether, over decades, it produces the ecosystem we described in `MANIFESTO.md`. That is the only test that matters.
