# Content Policy

## What Our Products Publish, Cite, and Represent

This document governs the scope of Islamic scholarly content produced or surfaced by projects under the initiative — primarily Basira, but also any future product that presents Islamic religious material to users.

It answers the practical questions content producers and reviewers will face every day:
- Which sources are in scope?
- How is scholarly disagreement represented?
- How are edge cases resolved?
- How is content verified before publication?

Content policy complements but does not replace the principles in `PRINCIPLES.md` or the non-negotiables in `docs/07-non-negotiables.md`. Where there is tension, the principles win.

---

## Scope of Islamic Scholarly Content

### In scope

The initiative's Islamic-content products operate within the scope of **mainstream Islamic scholarly tradition** as recognized across Sunni and Shia institutions. This includes:

**Primary sources:**
- The Quran in its canonical reading and in the ten authenticated variant readings (`qira'at`).
- Sunni hadith collections including — but not limited to — Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Sunan Abu Dawud, Jami' at-Tirmidhi, Sunan an-Nasa'i, Sunan Ibn Majah, Muwatta Malik, Musnad Ahmad.
- Shia hadith collections including — but not limited to — Al-Kafi, Man La Yahduruhu al-Faqih, Tahdhib al-Ahkam, Al-Istibsar, Nahj al-Balagha.
- Ibadi hadith collections (notably Musnad ar-Rabi').

**Classical scholarship:**
- Major works of tafseer from multiple schools (Ibn Kathir, Tabari, Qurtubi, Razi, al-Mizan, Majma' al-Bayan, and others).
- Foundational fiqh texts of each recognized school (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali, Ja'fari, Zaydi, Ibadi).
- Usul al-Fiqh (jurisprudential methodology) across the recognized schools.
- Seerah (prophetic biography) from multiple classical sources.
- Aqidah texts within the Sunni and Shia theological traditions (Ash'ari, Maturidi, Athari, Twelver Shia, Zaydi, and others).

**Contemporary scholarship:**
- Works produced by scholars operating within the methodological continuity of the above traditions — Azhari graduates, Qom-trained scholars, Deobandi scholars, Barelvi scholars, Ahl al-Hadith scholars, Ja'fari jurists, Salafi scholars within the tradition, and others who are recognized as operating within mainstream Islamic scholarly tradition.
- Major contemporary fatwa councils (e.g. European Council for Fatwa and Research, Dar al-Ifta al-Misriyya, Grand Marja' offices).

### The boundary of scope

The scope above is defined **positively**. Sources and positions that fall within it are in scope. Sources outside it are simply outside — not because of hostility toward any individual or group, but because the initiative's scholarly products are a window into the Islamic scholarly tradition as recognized by that tradition itself.

In practical terms: our content scope is bounded by the recognitions established across major Sunni, Shia, and Ibadi institutions — such as Al-Azhar, the offices of Shia marja' al-taqlid, Darul Uloom Deoband and Nadwat al-Ulama, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation's scholarly bodies, the Muslim World League, the International Islamic Fiqh Academy, and analogous institutions.

When a new question arises about whether a specific source, figure, or text is within scope, the question is resolved by reference to the scholarly consensus of these institutions — not by our independent judgment. We do not adjudicate Islamic theological boundaries. We reflect them.

### Out of scope (without hostility)

Certain categories of material are outside the scope of products whose purpose is to represent Islamic scholarly tradition. Being out of scope does not mean being discussed hostilely; it simply means not being presented as Islamic scholarly content. Examples:

- Non-Islamic religious traditions (Christianity, Judaism, Hinduism, Buddhism, etc.). These may be discussed factually where context calls for it, but our products do not present them as Islamic scholarship.
- Secular academic critiques of Islam may be discussed in the context of scholarly responses, but are not presented as Islamic scholarly content themselves.
- Fringe or novel movements whose relationship to Islamic scholarly tradition is not recognized by the institutions listed above.

Users interested in these materials are served by other tools. Our tools stay within their scope.

### Academic and contextual use

For users with research needs (academic study of Islam by non-Muslim scholars, comparative religion, journalism), our tools will — where configured for such use — discuss material outside the primary scope **as academic or contextual reference**, clearly labeled as such. This does not expand the scope of what we present *as Islamic scholarship*.

## Representing Scholarly Disagreement

Muslim scholars disagree on many questions. Our products do not paper over disagreement.

### The default rule

When scholars disagree on a question, our output:

1. **Names the question as contested.**
2. **Presents the positions of the major schools that hold distinct views**, with their reasoning in summary form.
3. **Cites sources for each position.**
4. **Does not endorse one position as "the Muslim position"** when there is genuine scholarly disagreement.
5. **Recommends consultation with qualified scholars** for users seeking personal guidance rather than informational overview.

### Filter features for institutional users

Madaris, mosques, universities, and other institutional users may have a legitimate interest in configuring products to emphasize a specific madhhab for their students or congregants (a Maliki institution serving Maliki students, for example).

Such configuration is permitted as a **user-controlled feature**, never as a default that hides scholarly pluralism. The feature must:
- Be explicitly configured by the institution.
- Clearly indicate to users when output is filtered.
- Allow users to switch to a full-spectrum view at any time.
- Never be used to produce content that falsely claims "the Muslim position" excludes other schools.

### What we do not do

We do not:
- Present a single school's position as universal when it is not.
- Erase minority scholarly opinions because they are minority.
- Fabricate consensus where none exists.
- Allow commercial or political pressure to bias representation.

## Scholar Verification

Content produced or surfaced by our products that claims scholarly authority must be verified by qualified scholars before publication. The process:

### Scholar eligibility

A scholar qualifying to verify content for our products must have:

- Formal training in an established Islamic scholarly institution (ijazah, degree from Al-Azhar or equivalent, recognized seminary completion, or equivalent traditional-chain qualification).
- Demonstrated contemporary scholarly practice (teaching, publishing, issuing fatawa, or serving in recognized religious roles).
- Willingness to operate under the initiative's principles — particularly non-sectarianism in reviewing content from traditions other than one's own.

Institutional affiliation is not required. Independent scholars with credible qualifications are welcomed.

### Scholar compensation

Scholars who provide substantive verification are compensated. Relying on unpaid scholarly labor is extractive and unsustainable.

Compensation is structured as:
- **Hourly or per-review rates** for specific review work.
- **Retainers** for scholars who provide ongoing advisory consultation.
- **Endowment-style support** for senior scholars whose general scholarly work indirectly benefits the project.

Rates are published. Conflicts of interest are disclosed.

### Review workflow

For each unit of scholarly content (a new entry in a knowledge base, a new school's position being added, a user-facing explanation of a contested question):

1. **Draft** produced by contributors (human, AI-assisted, or AI with human review).
2. **Primary review** by at least one scholar within the tradition of the content (e.g. Shafi'i fiqh content reviewed by a Shafi'i-trained scholar).
3. **Cross-school review** for content that touches multiple traditions, by a scholar from each relevant tradition.
4. **Record of review** — scholar, date, any changes requested — stored with the content.
5. **Periodic re-review** as the corpus grows and standards evolve.

### Rejection and appeal

Scholars may reject content as:
- Inaccurate (citations wrong, positions misrepresented).
- Incomplete (significant positions omitted).
- Insufficiently sourced.
- Methodologically flawed.

Rejection does not mean the content is permanently excluded — it means it returns to drafting. Appeals are resolved by senior scholars or advisory council review.

### Transparency of review

The identities of verifying scholars are typically published, with their consent. Scholars in jurisdictions where association with this work poses risks may operate pseudonymously. In all cases, the *fact* that review occurred, and the *credentials category* of the reviewer, are disclosed.

## Quranic Content

The Quran is treated with particular care.

- The canonical Arabic text is presented accurately, in `Rasm Uthmani` or standard modern orthography as appropriate.
- Translations are clearly labeled as translations, with translator and edition.
- Multiple translations are available where valuable; no single translation is presented as "the translation."
- Variant readings (`qira'at`) are treated as the scholarly tradition treats them — authenticated readings represented accurately, with provenance.
- Tafseer is cited with attribution to the source work.
- No Quranic text is altered, paraphrased-as-if-original, or presented in a way that could mislead.

---

## Hadith Content

- Hadith are cited with collection, book, and number.
- Gradings (sahih, hasan, da'if, mawdu') are included where the source work provides them.
- Where a hadith is accepted in one tradition and not another, the difference is disclosed.
- Fabricated or severely weak (`mawdu'`) reports are discussed only in explicitly contextual/historical framings — they are never presented as authoritative.
- Chains of transmission (`isnad`) are available for serious scholarly use.

---

## Fatawa and Legal Content

- Fatawa are presented with scholar attribution, issuing body, date, and context.
- Our products do not issue new fatawa. We surface existing scholarly positions; we do not pronounce new ones.
- Users seeking personal guidance are referred to qualified scholars. Our tools are informational, not juridical.
- Where multiple fatawa exist on a question, representative ones are surfaced with clear indication of their plurality.

---

## Languages

- Arabic originals are always available for primary sources.
- Translations are clearly marked and attributed.
- Translation quality is itself a scholarly concern — we prefer established published translations over machine translation for sacred and scholarly text.
- Where machine translation is used for accessibility (e.g. summaries, user interface), this is labeled.
- Multi-language output maintains scholarly integrity in each language.

## Handling Edge Cases

Several categories recur as edge cases:

### Historical material that reflects its time
Classical texts may contain material that contemporary readers — Muslim and non-Muslim — find difficult. Our posture:
- Present classical sources accurately. Do not silently alter historical texts.
- Provide scholarly context where it illuminates meaning.
- When classical material is contested within contemporary scholarship, surface that contestation.

### Contemporary political content
Our products are not political commentators. When users ask about contemporary political questions:
- We surface relevant scholarly analysis where it exists.
- We do not substitute our judgment for scholars'.
- We are cautious about wading into partisan disputes where scholarly consensus is absent.

### Content about women, gender, and contemporary social issues
These topics involve both classical scholarship and significant contemporary discussion.
- Classical scholarship is presented with fidelity to its sources.
- Contemporary scholarly work addressing these topics is also presented where relevant.
- Where contemporary scholars within the tradition have developed new perspectives, these are surfaced as such — not as overriding classical scholarship, but as part of the live tradition.

### Content about non-Muslim traditions
- Accurate information where relevant.
- Respectful presentation — we do not disparage other traditions.
- Clear framing that our products are resources on Islamic scholarship, not authoritative sources on other religions.

### Content about individuals whose status is contested
Occasionally a figure's classification — scholar, heretic, innovator — is contested among Muslims. Our practice:
- Present the scholarly debate accurately.
- Cite representative positions.
- Do not adjudicate the debate ourselves.

## What We Will Not Produce

Regardless of user request or commercial incentive:

- Fabricated hadith.
- Fatawa attributed to scholars who did not issue them.
- Misrepresentations of one school's position by another school's standards.
- Content that promotes takfir (excommunication) of other Muslims.
- Content that incites violence against any group.
- Content that dehumanizes any religious, ethnic, or national group.
- Content that misrepresents the Quran's plain meaning for polemical purposes.

These are non-negotiable and are grounds for refusing requests.

## Governance of Content Policy

This policy is reviewed at least annually. Significant changes follow the governance process in `docs/06-governance.md`, with particular care to consult the scholarly advisory council.

Specific questions about the scope or interpretation of this policy are resolved:
1. First by reference to this document.
2. Then by the scholar verification lead(s).
3. Then by the advisory council.
4. For fundamental questions, through the full governance process.

---

## Closing

The Islamic scholarly tradition is vast, plural, and alive. Our products exist to make it more accessible, not to flatten or reshape it.

Our content policy is therefore an act of stewardship: we hold the tradition as it is, with fidelity, and offer it to those who seek it.

If we get specific decisions wrong, we correct them — transparently, with the relevant scholars, and in service of users rather than any institution.

*Wa Allahu a'lam* — and Allah knows best.
