# Glossary

## Arabic, Islamic, and Technical Terms Used in This Repository

This glossary explains terms that appear across the foundation documents. Entries are listed by transliteration. Arabic script is provided where relevant. Sources are cited where a term derives from a specific Quranic verse, hadith, or established scholarly usage.

This document is intended for every reader — Muslim or not, Arabic-speaking or not — to follow the reasoning of the foundation documents without external lookup.

## Core Islamic Concepts

### Allah (الله)
The Arabic word for God. Used by Muslims and by Arabic-speaking Christians and Jews. Derived from `al-ilah` (the God). Central to Islamic theology is the oneness of Allah (`tawhid`).

### Ummah (أمة)
The global community of Muslims, considered as a single community regardless of nation, ethnicity, or sect. The term appears in the Quran in multiple contexts. In this repository, "Ummah" refers to this global Muslim community.

### Islam (الإسلام)
Literally "submission" — to the will of Allah. The religion revealed through the prophets, culminating with the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him.

### Muslim (مسلم)
One who submits to Allah — a follower of Islam. Throughout these documents, "Muslim" is used in its mainstream scholarly sense — those recognized as such within the long tradition of Sunni and Shia scholarly consensus.

### Peace be upon him (عليه الصلاة والسلام)
A prayer invoked after mentioning a prophet, especially Prophet Muhammad. Commonly written as (saw) or (PBUH) in English texts. We use "peace be upon him" spelled out for accessibility.

## Values and Virtues

### Adl (عدل)
Justice. A central Quranic concept (e.g. Quran 4:58, 5:8, 16:90). Acting with fairness, establishing equity. In these documents, `adl` is the disposition that shapes decisions about who to serve and how.

### Amanah (أمانة)
Trust, stewardship. What is entrusted to a person for safekeeping. Quran 33:72 describes `al-amanah` as a weighty trust that the heavens and earth declined to carry before humans accepted it. In this repository, knowledge and the safety of users are treated as forms of amanah.

### Haqq (حق)
Truth, right, reality. Both "what is true" and "what is owed." Related: *huquq* (rights).

### Hifz (حفظ)
Preservation, protection, memorization. Used in compound terms like `hifz al-ilm` (preservation of knowledge), `hifz al-nafs` (preservation of life), `hifz al-mustad'afin` (protection of the vulnerable).

### Hikmah (حكمة)
Wisdom. Practical wisdom that combines knowledge with right action. Quran 2:269 — *"He gives wisdom to whom He wills, and whoever has been given wisdom has certainly been given much good."*

### Ihsan (إحسان)
Excellence, beauty in action, doing something as if Allah is watching — because He is. From the famous Hadith of Gabriel (Sahih Muslim): *"Ihsan is to worship Allah as though you see Him, and if you cannot see Him, then indeed He sees you."*

### Ikhlas (إخلاص)
Sincerity of intention. Acting for the sake of Allah rather than for praise, power, or personal gain. Surah 112 (Al-Ikhlas) is named for this concept.

### Karamah (كرامة)
Dignity. `Karamat al-insan` — the inherent dignity of the human being. Quran 17:70 — *"We have honored the children of Adam."*

### Mustad'afin (المستضعفين)
The oppressed, the vulnerable, those rendered weak by circumstance. Quran 4:75 commands Muslims to fight for "the oppressed among men, women, and children." `Hifz al-mustad'afin` (protection of the vulnerable) is a core principle of this initiative.

### Rahmah (رحمة)
Mercy, compassion. Every chapter of the Quran (except one) begins with invocation of Allah as `ar-Rahman` (the Merciful) and `ar-Rahim` (the Compassionate).

### Sabr (صبر)
Patience, steadfastness, endurance. A recurring Quranic virtue. Quran 2:153 — *"Indeed, Allah is with the patient."*

### Shahadah (شهادة)
Witness, testimony. Two primary meanings: (1) the declaration of faith, (2) bearing witness to truth. The root `sh-h-d` appears throughout the Quran in contexts of justice and truth-telling. The project `Shahid` (witness) draws its name from this concept.

### Tawhid (توحيد)
The oneness and uniqueness of Allah. The central theological doctrine of Islam.

### Wahdah (وحدة)
Unity. `Wahdat al-ummah` — unity of the Muslim community.

### Wuduh (وضوح)
Clarity. Used in these documents for radical transparency — the principle that decisions should be visible.

## Institutions and Legal Forms

### Bayt al-Mal (بيت المال)
Treasury. The public treasury in historical Islamic governance.

### Fatwa (فتوى) / Fatawa (plural)
A scholarly legal opinion issued in response to a specific question. Non-binding in most legal systems but authoritative for the inquirer within that scholar's framework.

### Ijma' (إجماع)
Consensus of qualified scholars on a legal or theological matter. One of the classical sources of Islamic law alongside Quran, Sunnah, and Qiyas (analogical reasoning).

### Ijtihad (اجتهاد)
Independent scholarly reasoning on legal matters, especially where explicit textual guidance is not available. Qualified practitioners are called `mujtahids`.

### Khilafah (خلافة)
Caliphate. Also used in the general sense of human stewardship of the earth (Quran 2:30).

### Madrasa (مدرسة) / Madaris (plural)
A school. In contemporary usage, often specifically an Islamic religious school. Madaris are a major institutional form throughout Muslim history.

### Maqasid al-Shariah (مقاصد الشريعة)
The higher objectives of Islamic law. Classically summarized as the preservation of: religion (`din`), life (`nafs`), intellect (`aql`), lineage (`nasl`), and property (`mal`). Some scholars add dignity (`ird`) and others.

### Shariah (شريعة)
The religious law of Islam, derived from the Quran, Sunnah, and scholarly methodology.

### Shura (شورى)
Consultation. Quran 42:38 praises those "whose affair is [determined by] consultation among themselves." The principle of shura is foundational to Islamic governance theory.

### Waqf (وقف) / Awqaf (plural)
A charitable endowment. Property dedicated perpetually to a public or religious purpose; the principal cannot be sold or reassigned, but the proceeds fund the purpose in perpetuity. Historically funded mosques, schools, hospitals, water infrastructure, and care for the poor.

## Sources and Scholarship

### Quran (القرآن)
The central religious text of Islam, believed by Muslims to be the literal word of Allah revealed to the Prophet Muhammad. Cited in these documents by `Surah Name Chapter:Verse` format.

### Sunnah (السنة)
The example and practice of the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, as recorded in hadith collections. Second-primary source of Islamic law after the Quran.

### Hadith (حديث) / Ahadith (plural)
A report attributed to the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, documenting his sayings, actions, or tacit approvals. Hadiths are graded by authenticity. Major collections vary by tradition.

### Tafseer (تفسير)
Exegesis, commentary on the Quran. Classical works include Tafseer Ibn Kathir, Tafseer al-Tabari, Tafseer al-Qurtubi, Tafseer al-Razi, Mafatih al-Ghayb, and many others.

### Seerah (سيرة)
The biography of the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him. Classical works include Ibn Ishaq's Seerah and Ibn Hisham's edition.

### Fiqh (فقه)
Islamic jurisprudence — the scholarly tradition of deriving legal rulings from sources. Different schools have different fiqh methodologies.

### Usul al-Fiqh (أصول الفقه)
The principles and methodology of jurisprudence — the meta-discipline that governs how fiqh is derived.

### Aqidah (عقيدة)
Islamic creed, theology. The systematic statement of what Muslims believe.

### Sahih (صحيح)
"Authentic" — the highest grade of hadith authenticity in classification systems.

### Major Hadith Collections (Sunni)
- **Sahih al-Bukhari** (d. 870 CE)
- **Sahih Muslim** (d. 875 CE)
- **Sunan Abu Dawud** (d. 889 CE)
- **Jami' at-Tirmidhi** (d. 892 CE)
- **Sunan an-Nasa'i** (d. 915 CE)
- **Sunan Ibn Majah** (d. 887 CE)

The first two together are called *as-Sahihan*; the six together are *al-Kutub as-Sittah* (the Six Books).

### Major Hadith Collections (Shia)
- **Al-Kafi** by al-Kulayni (d. 941 CE)
- **Man La Yahduruhu al-Faqih** by al-Saduq (d. 991 CE)
- **Tahdhib al-Ahkam** by Shaykh al-Tusi (d. 1067 CE)
- **Al-Istibsar** by Shaykh al-Tusi

Together called *al-Kutub al-Arba'ah* (the Four Books).

## Jurisprudential Schools and Traditions

### Madhhab (مذهب) / Madhahib (plural)
A school of jurisprudence. Scholars within a madhhab share a methodology for deriving rulings.

### Major Sunni Madhahib
- **Hanafi** — founded by Imam Abu Hanifa (d. 767 CE). Largest school globally.
- **Maliki** — founded by Imam Malik (d. 795 CE). Predominant in North and West Africa.
- **Shafi'i** — founded by Imam al-Shafi'i (d. 820 CE). Predominant in Southeast Asia, East Africa, parts of Levant.
- **Hanbali** — founded by Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d. 855 CE). Predominant in Arabian Peninsula.

### Major Shia Madhahib
- **Ja'fari** (Twelver / Ithna Ashari) — largest Shia school. Associated with Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq (d. 765 CE).
- **Zaydi** — predominant historically in Yemen.
- **Isma'ili** (including Nizari and Musta'li branches) — various global communities.

### Ibadi (إباضي)
A third major tradition, predominant in Oman and parts of East and North Africa. Neither Sunni nor Shia in the narrow sense; arose from early Khariji movements but distinct.

### Other Streams and Movements
- **Sufi / Tasawwuf** — the tradition of Islamic spirituality and mysticism. Not a separate sect; many Sunnis and Shias identify with Sufi orders (`tariqah`).
- **Salafi** — a Sunni reform movement emphasizing return to the methodology of the first three generations (`as-salaf as-salih`).
- **Ash'ari / Maturidi** — classical Sunni theological schools.
- **Athari** — a theological approach associated with traditionalist Sunni scholars.

## Historical Figures and Places

### Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him (c. 570–632 CE)
The final prophet in Islam. Revealed the Quran over 23 years. Established the first Muslim community in Medina.

### The Four Rightly-Guided Caliphs (Rashidun)
The first four successors to the Prophet in Sunni accounts: Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali. Shia accounts differ on the status of the first three, recognizing Ali as the rightful immediate successor.

### Fatima al-Fihri (9th century CE)
Muslim woman who founded the University of Al-Qarawiyyin in Fez, Morocco, in 859 CE — recognized as the world's oldest continuously operating university.

### Al-Khwarizmi (c. 780–850 CE)
Mathematician, astronomer, geographer. Working at Bayt al-Hikmah. The word "algorithm" derives from his name.

### Ibn Sina / Avicenna (980–1037 CE)
Physician, philosopher, polymath. His *Canon of Medicine* was a standard medical text for centuries across Muslim and European civilizations.

### Ibn al-Haytham / Alhazen (c. 965–1040 CE)
Mathematician, astronomer, physicist. Foundational to optics and to the scientific method.

### Al-Biruni (973–1048 CE)
Astronomer, mathematician, geographer, historian. Measured the Earth's radius with remarkable precision.

### Ibn Rushd / Averroes (1126–1198 CE)
Jurist, philosopher, physician. Preserved and extended Aristotelian philosophy.

### Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406 CE)
Historian, sociologist, economist. His *Muqaddimah* is considered a founding text of sociology.

### Salah ad-Din al-Ayyubi / Saladin (1137–1193 CE)
Sultan who unified Egypt and Syria, recovered Jerusalem in 1187 after a twenty-year institutional and military buildup.

### Bayt al-Hikmah (بيت الحكمة) — The House of Wisdom
Library, translation bureau, and research academy in Baghdad, c. 8th–13th centuries CE. Center of medieval Islamic scholarship.

### Al-Azhar (الأزهر)
University and mosque in Cairo, founded 970 CE. One of the most influential institutions in Sunni Islamic scholarship.

### Al-Qarawiyyin (القرويين)
University in Fez, founded 859 CE by Fatima al-Fihri. World's oldest continuously operating university.

## Technical and AI Terms

### AI — Artificial Intelligence
Broadly, computational systems that perform tasks associated with human cognition. In this repository, usually refers specifically to modern machine-learning systems, especially large language models.

### LLM — Large Language Model
A machine-learning model trained on large text corpora to generate, classify, or analyze text. Examples at time of writing: GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Mistral, Qwen, Jais, Falcon, Fanar, Noor.

### RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation
A technique combining a knowledge retrieval step with a generative model. Enables grounded, citation-based responses rather than reliance on model parameters alone. Central to Basira's design.

### Fine-Tuning
Further training a pre-trained model on a specific dataset to specialize its capability.

### Embedding
A numerical representation of text (or other data) in high-dimensional space that captures semantic meaning. Used for retrieval and similarity.

### Tokenization
Breaking text into units ("tokens") for model processing. Classical Arabic has tokenization challenges most general tokenizers handle poorly.

### C2PA — Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity
An open technical standard for cryptographically signing and tracking the provenance of digital content. Relevant to deepfake detection and content verification. https://c2pa.org

### OPSEC — Operational Security
The discipline of protecting information that, if exposed, could compromise a person or operation. Relevant to counter-surveillance tooling.

### Zero-Knowledge Architecture
A system design where operators cannot access the data they process — typically via client-side encryption. Protects users even if the operator is compromised or compelled.

### CLOUD Act
U.S. legislation permitting U.S. authorities to compel U.S.-based cloud providers to disclose data regardless of where it is stored. Relevant to jurisdiction selection.

### Deepfake
Synthetic media — images, audio, or video — generated or manipulated by AI, often to depict events that did not occur.

## Notes on Transliteration

We use a readable transliteration of Arabic rather than a strict scholarly transliteration system. For example, we write "Shariah" rather than "Sharīʿah", and "Salah ad-Din" rather than "Ṣalāḥ ad-Dīn". This is for accessibility — most readers do not need the diacritical precision, and those who do will recognize the term either way.

Where Arabic script is included, it appears in parentheses alongside the transliteration.
