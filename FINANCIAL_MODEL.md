# Financial Model

## Who Earns What, and Why Contributors Can Trust This

This document makes the money flow explicit. Vague financial commitments are how mission-driven organizations drift into extractive ones. By publishing the model — including founder compensation, revenue allocation, and acquisition scenarios — we make drift observable.

**Model chosen: Option 2 — Capped Founder Equity + Waqf Commitment.**

> **Note on specific numbers.** Figures marked `[TENTATIVE]` are founder-proposed starting points that will be ratified in the legal entity's founding documents. Once ratified, they are **contractually binding** and not changeable except through the governance process described in `docs/06-governance.md`.

---

## Core Commitment (the thing that can't change)

Three commitments bind every other decision in this document. They are intended to be written into the legal entity's articles of incorporation / bylaws and made non-amendable except through supermajority governance review:

1. **Founder's lifetime financial return from the initiative is capped.** No matter how successful the commercial entity becomes, the founder does not receive unlimited equity upside. Above the cap, all additional value flows to mission work.

2. **No contributor is extracted from.** Open-source contributors retain full license to their work. Scholarly contributors are paid. Significant external contributions are eligible for bounties or paid roles. Nobody's unpaid work becomes someone else's private wealth.

3. **The commercial entity exists to fund the mission, not the reverse.** If a choice arises between commercial growth and mission integrity, mission wins.

## Revenue Allocation (Annual, Net of Operating Costs)

Percentages below are **of net revenue** (gross revenue minus direct operating costs: hosting, APIs, payment processors, legal, accounting, security). The goal is to make the allocation legible at a glance and enforceable as the entity scales.

| Bucket | Share of Net Revenue | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Team salaries (fair market)** | 30-50% | Scales with headcount; published ranges |
| **Scholar compensation** | 10-15% | Paid per verification + retainers |
| **Mission allocation (Track B)** | 20% minimum (floor) | Legally binding; audited; rolls forward in loss years |
| **Contributor bounty / grant pool** | 5% | Bounties for significant external contributions |
| **Reserve / runway** | 5-10% | Cash buffer; limits to prevent hoarding |
| **Founder compensation (within cap)** | residual | Salary + capped upside; transparent |

**If revenue is insufficient** to cover all buckets, priority order:

1. Infrastructure (services stay up — our users depend on us)
2. Scholar compensation (commitments to people must be honored)
3. Team salaries (people can't eat principles)
4. Mission allocation (the floor is the floor; it rolls forward as arrears)
5. Founder compensation (takes hit first when we're under pressure)

This priority order is structural — written into operating policy. Founder cannot take compensation while scholar invoices are unpaid.

## Founder Compensation

### Monthly salary

Fair-market salary for the role, published, and within a reasonable multiple of the lowest-paid full-time team member.

**[TENTATIVE] Starting range (solo phase, Pakistan-based):** $3,000 – $5,000 USD / month.
**[TENTATIVE] Scaled range (once sustainable, team of 5-10):** $6,000 – $10,000 USD / month.
**[TENTATIVE] Long-run ceiling:** equivalent to senior-engineer rate in the founder's region, not startup-CEO rate.

Adjusted for inflation via a defined annual review.

Published annually in the transparency report.

### Lifetime cap (the core of Option 2)

The founder's **total lifetime financial return** from the initiative — including accumulated salary above a baseline, any bonus, any equity-style payout, and any acquisition proceeds — is capped at:

**[TENTATIVE] $500,000 USD lifetime, indexed to inflation.**

This figure is deliberately **small** — enough to matter, small enough to make clear that the initiative is not a founder-wealth vehicle:

- **Enough** to provide a modest retirement-style cushion for the founder over a multi-decade tenure.
- **Not remotely Silicon-Valley-founder wealth.** At $500K over a 20-25 year tenure, the cap works out to roughly $20-25K/year above salary — comparable to an ordinary retirement contribution.
- **Indexed** so a successful entity 20 years from now does not quietly make the cap meaningless.
- **Small by design.** The founder chose this structure deliberately. A "generous" cap would undermine the signal that this is not a startup exit.

**What the cap includes:**
- Salary above a defined "baseline salary for comparable non-profit leadership" — excess counts toward the cap.
- Performance bonuses.
- Any founder-specific equity, shares, or revenue-share payouts.
- Acquisition proceeds directly attributable to the founder.
- Severance above standard market.

**What the cap does NOT include:**
- Fair-market baseline salary (within published ranges).
- Reimbursed business expenses.
- Retirement contributions at standard employer rates.
- Speaking fees, consulting, or other work genuinely outside the initiative's scope.

### Above the cap

Every dollar the founder would otherwise receive above the cap flows to mission work and is documented publicly. The founder cannot accumulate debt that is forgiven later; the cap is hard.

### If the founder leaves

- No severance beyond standard market.
- No retained equity above what has already counted toward the cap.
- No "golden parachute" on acquisition.
- If founder is still under cap at departure, remaining capacity is forfeited (not paid out as exit).

### If the founder dies or is incapacitated

- Unused cap capacity goes to mission work, not heirs, by default.
- Founder may designate up to 25% of unused cap capacity to heirs in a documented testament (acknowledging family responsibilities).
- Remaining 75% goes to mission.

This is stricter than most "benefit corporation" structures and is deliberately so. The initiative is bigger than any founder.

## Team Compensation

- **Fair market salaries** for roles, benchmarked against comparable positions in the founder's / team-member's jurisdiction.
- **Published ranges** per role tier.
- **Salary multiple cap:** highest-paid team member earns no more than [TENTATIVE] 5× lowest-paid full-time team member. Forces equity from the top.
- **No equity grants** to employees that confer upside beyond salary. (We are not a startup; we do not have an exit.)
- **Bonus pool** allocated when annual targets exceeded; distributed by transparent criteria, not founder discretion.
- **Retirement contributions** at standard employer rates.

Pakistani, Indian, Bangladeshi, Indonesian, Malaysian team members are paid at rates that make sense in their markets — not undercut to "local rates" because we can get away with it. Fair across geographies.

## Contributor Compensation Tiers

The question "if I contribute, what do I get?" deserves honest answers for each contribution type.

### Tier 1 — Paid roles

- **Full-time team members.** Fair-market salary. Standard employment.
- **Scholar advisors.** Per-review, retainer, or grant structures.
- **Contracted specialists.** Specific scope, specific pay (security auditors, translators on substantive work, legal counsel, accountants).
- **Commissioned content creators** (when we pay for specific curation work).

### Tier 2 — Bounty / grant recipients

When an external contributor produces substantive work that the initiative benefits from:

- **Feature bounties** for significant agreed-in-advance work. Published bounty program once infrastructure exists.
- **Retroactive grants** for already-completed exceptional contributions. Reviewed case-by-case.
- **Conference / research travel grants** for contributors presenting work on the initiative.

Target: 5% of net revenue flows through this pool.

### Tier 3 — Open-source contributors (the most common case)

If you submit a pull request, open an issue, or contribute documentation / translation / testing / code review without a paid arrangement:

**What you explicitly receive:**

- **Full retained license to your own work.** Your contribution is licensed under MIT / CC BY-SA 4.0 (per file type) — which means **you can use it anywhere, forever, including in your own commercial products.**
- **Public attribution** (unless you request pseudonymity). Credited in release notes, contributor lists, and the permanent git history.
- **Right to fork.** Per Principle 8, you can always take the open components and build separately.
- **Reference value.** Demonstrated contribution to a serious project — useful for your career, academic work, scholarly reputation.
- **Network access.** Contributors engage with scholars, technologists, and institutions you might not otherwise reach.
- **Mission ownership.** You participated in building Ummah digital infrastructure. This is not rhetorical — your commits are in the public record.

**What you do NOT automatically receive:**

- Equity in the commercial entity.
- Revenue share.
- A salary.

**But:** if your contribution is substantial, the Tier-2 bounty/grant process exists, and paid roles are regularly offered to proven contributors. Nobody is forced to stay in the unpaid tier if they want paid work and have demonstrated capacity.

### Tier 4 — Institutional partners

Partners (universities, research institutions, NGOs, Islamic scholarly bodies) that commit sustained collaboration:

- **No money flows to partners.** Mission-aligned partners collaborate as peers.
- **Shared credit** on joint work.
- **Access** to our corpora and tooling on preferred terms for their institutional use.
- **Coordinated influence** — partners have voice in governance (via advisory roles) proportional to their engagement.

## What About Forks and Competitors?

Per Principle 8 (Fork Rights):

If a developer takes our open-source code and builds a commercial product that competes with Basira — **we welcome that.** The mission is not our market share; the mission is a thriving Muslim AI ecosystem.

If other Muslim AI companies emerge using our open infrastructure:
- **We do not attempt to restrict their use.**
- **We do not sue them.**
- **We see them as success**, not competition.

Our competitive advantage is: trust with scholars, verified data curation, and operational excellence. These are not in the code. Someone forking the code does not take them.

## Legal Enforcement

Commitments in this document become real only if enforced legally. The steps:

### At founding (Phase 0 completion)

- Legal entity incorporated in a jurisdiction that allows the structure (UAE DIFC, Malaysia, Estonia, or equivalent — to be decided).
- Articles of incorporation / bylaws include:
  - Mission statement anchored to `PRINCIPLES.md`.
  - Founder cap figure (current: `[TENTATIVE] $1M USD indexed`).
  - Mission allocation floor (current: 20% of net revenue).
  - Acquisition clause (see below).
  - Governance process for any changes (supermajority + mission guardian approval).

### Annual enforcement

- **Independent audit** — external auditor reviews finances, verifies allocation percentages, confirms founder within cap.
- **Published transparency report** — revenue, costs, allocations, founder comp, team comp ranges, scholar comp totals.
- **Mission allocation verified** — the 20% floor is met, and how it was spent is documented.

### Change process

- Changes to founder cap, mission allocation floor, or acquisition clause require:
  - Public proposal with minimum 30-day comment period.
  - Majority of board including at least two mission guardians (see `docs/06-governance.md`).
  - Public justification.
- Changes cannot be made retroactively.
- Founder cannot unilaterally alter their own cap.

## Acquisition Scenario

### The entity is NOT for sale.

This is a hard commitment, stronger than a typical "we only sell on principled terms" clause. **The commercial entity cannot be sold, acquired, or merged under any circumstances short of the full governance-level dissolution process.**

### What this means structurally

- Articles of incorporation include a **non-transferability clause** preventing sale of the entity as a whole.
- Founder equity / ownership (within the cap) **cannot be sold to a third party** — it can only be relinquished back to the entity itself.
- Any offer of acquisition is **declined categorically**, not considered case-by-case.
- This constraint is **more durable than ordinary board decisions** — amending it requires the full non-negotiable amendment process (which is effectively impossible; see `docs/07-non-negotiables.md`).

### Why this strict?

Most "mission-driven" companies eventually sell. Principles soften. Boards get pressured. Founders tire. A capped-founder-equity structure alone is not sufficient protection, because an acquirer can simply buy out the founder at the cap and then change the mission.

A categorical no-sale commitment prevents that entire failure mode. The initiative's infrastructure cannot be redirected by a new owner because there will be no new owner.

### What about exits for the founder?

The founder's financial return is the **capped compensation and accumulated bonus within the cap**, paid out over time — not an acquisition lump sum. No "exit event" exists in this model. The founder's return is realized through ordinary salary and bonus over their tenure.

### What about mergers or partnerships?

- **Partnerships** are welcome — formal collaborations where each entity retains its identity and governance.
- **Mergers** are not — combining the entity with another under shared ownership is covered by the no-sale clause.
- **Spinoffs** (creating new entities for specific projects) are permitted and governed per `docs/06-governance.md`.

### What about dissolution?

If the initiative ever needs to wind down — because mission is complete, or because continuing operation has become impossible — dissolution proceeds are governed by the waqf structure:

- **All assets** (including the commercial entity's operating assets, if any) flow to mission work or to a successor mission-aligned entity.
- **No proceeds flow to founder, board members, or any private party** beyond compensation already accrued within the cap.
- **Community and advisory council** oversee the dissolution to prevent capture.

### Realistic scenarios

- **Offer from Muslim-world sovereign fund:** Declined. Even mission-aligned acquirers cannot acquire. Partnerships are the alternative.
- **Offer from frontier AI lab:** Declined. No circumstances change this.
- **Private equity:** Declined.
- **Pressure to sell during a financial crisis:** Declined. Dissolution with waqf distribution of assets is the emergency exit, not sale.

### In plain language

**This initiative is not for sale. Period. Not at any price, to any buyer, in any circumstance.** Anyone considering contributing, partnering, or investing time in this initiative can rely on that commitment.

## Transparency Commitments

Published annually in a Transparency Report:

- Gross revenue.
- Direct operating costs.
- Net revenue.
- Salary totals and ranges per tier.

## Why This Model Works (Honest Case)

**For the founder:**
- Real financial upside, bounded.
- Personal stake in success.
- Not financially ruinous to attempt.
- Clear ceiling means no hidden extraction temptation.

**For contributors:**
- They know the ceiling. Founder is not getting rich beyond a defined point.
- Their work is theirs (MIT license).
- Paths to paid work exist (bounties, grants, paid roles).
- The entity they contribute to cannot be sold out from under them.

**For scholars:**
- They are paid. Their work is not extracted.
- They have governance voice.
- Their credentials are protected, not exploited.

**For the mission:**
- At scale, the majority of value flows to mission work, not founder wealth.
- The model is stable across generations.
- Capture is structurally resisted.

## Why This Model Might Not Work (Honest Risks)

- **Founder burnout:** Solo founders with capped upside may burn out faster than unlimited-upside founders. Mitigation: sustainable pace, early team building, genuine ownership of mission as primary driver.
- **Harder to attract co-founders:** Traditional co-founders expect equity upside. Mitigation: attract co-founders who share the mission; offer them equivalent salary + cap structure.
- **Harder to raise traditional VC:** Most VCs require unlimited upside. Mitigation: we don't intend to raise traditional VC. Mission-aligned capital is what we seek.
- **Tempting to quietly amend later:** Future-me or future-board may be tempted to weaken the cap. Mitigation: structural governance barriers + public scrutiny + fork rights.

These are real risks. We document them so future contributors can evaluate honestly.

## FAQs

### "Can the cap be increased later?"
Only through the full governance process: public proposal, minimum 30-day comment period, supermajority board approval including mission guardians, published justification. Not a quiet founder decision.

### "$500K is still a lot — isn't that extractive?"
Reasonable disagreement. $500K over a 20-25 year founder tenure averages $20-25K/year above salary — comparable to a modest retirement contribution in most developed markets. The founder chose this smaller cap deliberately to signal that the initiative is not a wealth vehicle. If the community decides a lower cap is appropriate (e.g. $250K), that is acceptable. The principle is a cap; the exact figure remains open to community refinement before legal ratification.

### "Why not $0 founder equity (pure waqf)?"
We considered it. Option 1 (pure waqf) was rejected because solo-founder risk without any asymmetric upside is hard to sustain multi-decade. The cap is a compromise — enough to be sustainable, small enough not to betray the mission. If the community prefers Option 1, that is a governance decision that can be made.

### "What if inflation makes the cap meaningless in 20 years?"
Cap is indexed to USD inflation (or to a gold / basket reference if USD becomes unreliable). Indexing is a structural feature, not a discretionary adjustment.

### "Can the founder earn money on side work?"
Yes — consulting, speaking, writing, teaching outside the initiative's scope is not covered by the cap. The cap applies to returns **from the initiative specifically.**

### "What if you need to raise capital and the investor demands founder equity upside?"
Refused. Capital with such strings violates our governance. We seek capital aligned with these constraints or we do not take it.

### "What about team members — do they have similar caps?"
No individual cap for team members (salary caps aside). The team compensation structure is fair-market-without-equity-upside. The initiative does not create Silicon-Valley exits for anyone.

### "What if this entire model is wrong?"
Then it gets revised through governance. The cap and revenue allocation are material commitments — changing them requires justification, community input, and formal approval. The _principle_ of "no extractive founder exit" is harder to change — it is closer to a non-negotiable and would require rewriting `PRINCIPLES.md`, which is structurally difficult.
